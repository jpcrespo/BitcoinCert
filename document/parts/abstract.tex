\begin{abstract}

Document verification is a crucial part of detecting manipulation and fraud.
This process is traditionally centralized, making it costly, fragile, and a single point of failure for an issuing organization.
If the issuing organization is compromised, verifiability is lost.

In 2009, Bitcoin was deployed as a decentralized network with high security and availability.
"In addition to its use as a currency, it serves as a robust mechanism for anchoring immutable information.
Data anchoring methods on Bitcoin, such as \texttt{OP\_RETURN}, fake address or OpenTimeStamp, have limitations in scalability, privacy, and usability.
The Bitcoin protocol's Taproot improve in 2021 overcomes limitations on the base layer.

This work proposes a new document verification protocol leveraging Taproot's cryptographic tools.
It enables the verification of integrity, authenticity of issuance and validity revocation.
Using a Taproot address, it improves the scalability and privacy for a batch of $N$ documents.
The protocol is predicated on an off-chain verification model, minimizing the on-chain footprint. We rigorously analyze the computational overhead, providing detailed estimations of costs and transaction fees for efficient utilization of Bitcoin's block space.

\end{abstract}
