\section{Conclusion}

A decentralized certification protocol has advantages compared to traditional centralized systems.
Availability, denial-of-service resistance, and immutability security are characteristics of the Bitcoin network.
Methods studied in the literature show that Bitcoin has limitations regarding scalability, privacy, and applications in document verification.
Solutions like the use of \texttt{OP\_RETURN} and OpenTimeStamp protocols have their limitations.
With the Taproot upgrade operational on the network since 2021, Bitcoin overcomes these limitations.
It was identified that Taproot has not been studied in the literature for the use case of document verification.

The protocol proposed in this work leverages the cryptographic tools used in Taproot.
It allows integrity verification via the hash anchored in the Merkle tree and document ownership via Schnorr digital signatures.
It enables the revocation of document validity and proposes key rotation by chaining transactions that spend the previous output.
Preserving privacy by not revealing information from other documents in the batch.

The proposed protocol does not consider the validity of the content of the issued documents.
The revocation of validity remains the responsibility of the issuing organization.
It does not consider the preservation of the document and its proof of validity once issued and delivered to the end-user.
The study of long-term preservation and proof-of-validity mechanisms remains for future work.
As well as the implementation of penalties and incentives for users who present invalid documents as valid.
