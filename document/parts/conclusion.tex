\section{Conclusion}

Decentralized document verification protocols offer clear advantages over traditional centralized systems.
Properties such as high availability, resistance to denial-of-service attacks, and immutability are inherent to the Bitcoin network and make it a suitable foundation for long-term verification mechanisms.
However, existing approaches studied in the literature reveal that Bitcoin-based solutions have historically faced limitations in scalability, privacy, and expressiveness when applied to document verification.
Protocols such as \texttt{OP\_RETURN}-based anchoring and OpenTimestamps exhibit structural constraints that restrict their applicability to large-scale or privacy-sensitive use cases.

With the activation of the Taproot upgrade in 2021, Bitcoin introduced new cryptographic primitives that significantly expand its design space.
Despite these advancements, this work identifies that Taproot has not yet been explored in the literature as the basis for a complete decentralized document verification protocol.
This observation motivates the investigation proposed in this thesis.

The protocol presented in this work leverages Taproot’s cryptographic capabilities to support document integrity verification through Merkle tree commitments and issuer authenticity verification through Schnorr digital signatures.
It further enables document revocation through transaction chaining and supports signing key rotation without invalidating previously issued documents.
By anchoring documents in batches and avoiding explicit disclosure of auxiliary data, the protocol preserves privacy by preventing information leakage about other documents within the same batch.

This work explicitly limits its scope to cryptographic verification and does not address the semantic validity of document contents, which remains the responsibility of the issuing organization.
Additionally, long-term preservation of documents and validation proofs after issuance is not considered.
Future work includes the study of archival mechanisms for long-term proof persistence, enhanced revocation strategies, and incentive or penalty schemes for entities that attempt to present invalid or revoked documents as valid.
