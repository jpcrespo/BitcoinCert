INTRODUCTION PART

SLIDE 1 – DOCUMENT ISSUANCE AND VERIFICATION

Organizations issue documents to certify that a specific fact has occurred.
These documents can be physical, such as paper certificates,
or digital, such as PDF files.

Document verification is usually performed by the issuing organization itself.
This process typically involves checking three fundamental requirements:

The document content has not been altered.
The document was issued by the claimed organization.
The document has not been revoked.

--------------------------------------------------

SLIDE 2 – EXAMPLE: ACADEMIC CERTIFICATES

A common example of document verification is academic certificates.

Integrity:
the content, such as names, dates, or degrees, must not be modified.

Authenticity:
the certificate must be issued by a legitimate institution or university.

Validity:
the certificate may become invalid if it is revoked
due to errors or fraud discovered after issuance.

--------------------------------------------------

SLIDE 3 – DOCUMENT FRAUD AND CENTRALIZATION

Document fraud represents a multi-billion-dollar global industry.

Fraud in documents undermines trust and credibility
in issuing institutions.

In traditional systems,
document verification is centralized
and requires significant operational resources,
including personnel, infrastructure,
and physical anti-tampering measures.

In digital systems,
verification remains centralized
and introduces additional costs related to service availability
and cybersecurity,
such as denial-of-service protection
and server-side security services.

--------------------------------------------------

SLIDE 4 – BITCOIN BACKGROUND

In 2009, Bitcoin was introduced as a decentralized network,
secured by a proof-of-work consensus protocol
and elliptic curve cryptography.

Bitcoin exhibits several properties
that differentiate it from traditional web services:

Censorship resistance.
High availability through a globally distributed network of nodes,
as illustrated in the figure,
where each blue dot represents a Bitcoin node.
An immutable public ledger known as the blockchain.
Public verifiability without intermediaries.

--------------------------------------------------

SLIDE 5 – WHY BITCOIN

Bitcoin is deliberately designed
to minimize the attack surface of the network.

To reduce the risk of denial-of-service attacks,
Bitcoin limits on-chain computation.

It uses a stack-based scripting language
with a simple execution model.
It supports a small and well-defined set of instructions,
known as opcodes.
It does not allow loops, recursion,
or persistent memory.

Bitcoin follows the unspent transaction output,
or UTXO, model,
where each transaction is independently verified
by every node.

--------------------------------------------------

SLIDE 6 – OTHER BLOCKCHAIN PLATFORMS

Other blockchain platforms,
such as Ethereum or Solana,
adopt an account-based model
instead of the UTXO model.

These platforms support virtual machines
that enable expressive programs
with features such as loops,
recursion,
and complex state.

This design introduces a trade-off:

More expressive applications.
Higher transaction costs.
Larger attack surfaces,
which require security audits of smart contracts.
More complex consensus and governance mechanisms.

--------------------------------------------------

SLIDE 7 – TRANSITION TO THE PROPOSAL

In summary,
decentralized blockchain networks
enable new models for digital document verification.

Verification can be performed independently
of the issuing organization,
providing:

High availability.
Resistance to denial-of-service
and censorship attacks.
Immutable records
that preserve data integrity.
Prevention of repudiation
by the issuing entity.

LITERATURE REVIEW PART

SLIDE 1

The first step in using blockchain
for information preservation
was studied by Lemieux.

This work shows that blockchain
can provide integrity guarantees
for preserving digital records
in the short and medium term,
due to the use of cryptographic hash functions
and digital signatures.

The study also anticipates future risks
related to quantum computing,
which could potentially compromise
existing cryptographic schemes
such as ECDSA.

--------------------------------------------------

SLIDE 2

Bitcoin does not provide a native field
for embedding arbitrary metadata in transactions.

As a result,
alternative techniques have been proposed
to anchor data on-chain.

Gao studies a methodology
based on encoding data
into fake P2PKH Bitcoin addresses.

The entity constructing the transaction
does not control this address,
since it does not possess
the corresponding private key.

This technique allows embedding data
in multiples of 20 bytes per transaction.

However,
it permanently burns Bitcoin,
making it economically inefficient.

--------------------------------------------------

SLIDE 3

Bartoletti analyzes data embedding techniques in Bitcoin,
including fake addresses,
OP_RETURN,
and script-based methods.

Sward evaluates these methods
in terms of performance
and network health.

They conclude that OP_RETURN-based approaches
are preferable
because they do not bloat the UTXO set
and do not burn Bitcoin.

UTXO bloat refers to the growth
of the unspent transaction output set,
which must be stored in memory
by every node.

--------------------------------------------------

SLIDE 4

Strehle and Steinmetz analyze OP_RETURN usage
between 2018 and 2019.

The top 37 services account
for approximately 22 percent
of OP_RETURN usage,
contributing around 10 gigabytes of data
to the blockchain.

OP_RETURN places data
in the output script.

When the Bitcoin script interpreter
encounters OP_RETURN,
execution terminates immediately,
making the output provably unspendable.

--------------------------------------------------

SLIDE 5

OP_RETURN is the most widely used mechanism
for anchoring non-monetary data in Bitcoin.

It is adopted by protocols
such as Blockcerts
and OpenTimestamps.

The embedded data
is explicitly visible on-chain
and does not provide privacy guarantees.

OP_RETURN usage is constrained
by relay and mining policies,
such as the common 80-byte limit.

--------------------------------------------------

SLIDE 6

In 2017,
Segregated Witness, or SegWit,
was introduced
to improve the Bitcoin protocol.

SegWit separates signature data
from the transaction body
into a dedicated witness structure.

This enables larger data payloads
without increasing UTXO set growth.

--------------------------------------------------

SLIDE 7

In parallel,
other blockchain networks
introduced expressive smart contracts.

Several works use Ethereum
to attest certificates,
timestamps,
and structured data.

These approaches increase flexibility,
but also introduce higher costs,
greater complexity,
and larger attack surfaces.

--------------------------------------------------

SLIDE 8

The work
“Blockchain and Higher Education Diplomas”
identifies challenges
such as scalability,
error correction,
system complexity,
and lack of standards.

Rustemi finds that many platforms
are implemented on Ethereum
using complex smart contracts.

Revocation often remains a challenge
and may require third-party involvement.

--------------------------------------------------

SLIDE 9

After SegWit,
Bitcoin introduced new cryptographic primitives.

These include Schnorr signatures
and Merkleized Abstract Syntax Trees,
or MAST.

These features were activated in 2021
through the Taproot upgrade,
using a new output type
known as Pay-to-Taproot.

MOTIVATION PART

SLIDE 1

Based on the literature,
before Taproot,
building applications on Bitcoin
involved several limitations.

All existing methods
present technical and practical constraints.

--------------------------------------------------

SLIDE 2

Using another blockchain,
such as Ethereum,
involves a trade-off.

It increases the attack surface
and operational costs.

Complex executions
are more susceptible
to denial-of-service attacks.

--------------------------------------------------

SLIDE 3

Taproot evolves the Bitcoin protocol
by improving efficiency,
privacy,
and security.

Activated in 2021,
Taproot integrates MAST
and Schnorr signatures.

These properties motivate
revisiting document verification on Bitcoin.

METHODOLOGY PART

SLIDE 1

We propose a methodology
structured into two phases.

The first phase
is Protocol and Architecture Design.

The second phase
is Proof of Concept
and Performance Estimations.

--------------------------------------------------

SLIDE 2

The proposed protocol
supports three verification objectives:

Integrity.
Authenticity.
Revocation.

Verification is performed off-chain
using the document
and a control file.

--------------------------------------------------

SLIDE 3

Phase I includes issuance
by the organization
and verification
by a third party.

Information is anchored
in a single P2TR output.

Revocation is handled
through transaction chaining.

--------------------------------------------------

SLIDE 4

Privacy is preserved using MAST.

Each script leaf
is intentionally non-spendable.

Verification compares
off-chain reconstruction
with on-chain commitment.

--------------------------------------------------

SLIDE 5

Each Merkle leaf
contains the hash of a document.

All leaves
are combined
into a Merkle root.

--------------------------------------------------

SLIDE 6

Two key pairs are used.

One for Bitcoin transactions.
One for document signing.

--------------------------------------------------

SLIDE 7

Verification reconstructs
the Merkle root
and Taproot output.

Signatures
and revocation state
are checked.

--------------------------------------------------

SLIDE 8

Revocation is performed
by spending the Taproot output
and publishing a bitmap
in OP_RETURN.

--------------------------------------------------

SLIDE 9

The proof of concept
is executed
on Bitcoin Signet or Testnet.

--------------------------------------------------

SLIDE 10

Performance is evaluated
using scalability
and on-chain cost in vBytes.
